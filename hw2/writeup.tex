\section{Part A}

Throughout Part A, let \(\classA\) be the class of unions of at most two
intervals on the real line. For an ordered sample
\[
x_1 < x_2 < \dots < x_n,
\]
we write the induced labeling as a binary string
\[
(h(x_1),\dots,h(x_n)) \in \zeroone^n,
\]
where \(h(x_i)=1\) means \(x_i\) lies in the union of intervals and
\(h(x_i)=0\) means it does not.

\subsection{Problem A1}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
For an ordered sample \(x_1<\cdots<x_n\), characterize exactly which binary
labelings can be realized by a hypothesis in \(\classA\). The required answer
is that a labeling is realizable if and only if its string contains at most two
runs of \(1\)s.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The question asks for a structural description of the restriction patterns of
the class of unions of two intervals. The key concept is that an interval picks
out one contiguous block of sample points, so a union of two intervals can pick
out at most two such blocks. On a sorted sample, contiguous blocks are exactly
runs of \(1\)s in the binary labeling.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Prove the forward implication: every hypothesis in \(\classA\) induces
  at most two runs of \(1\)s.
  \item Prove the reverse implication: from any labeling with at most two runs
  of \(1\)s, explicitly construct one or two intervals that realize it.
  \item Conclude the desired equivalence.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(x_1<\dots<x_n\) be distinct real numbers. A binary labeling
\((y_1,\dots,y_n)\in\zeroone^n\) is realizable by \(\classA\) if and only if
the string \(y_1\dots y_n\) has at most two runs of \(1\)s.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First suppose \(h\in \classA\). Then there exist intervals \(I_1\) and \(I_2\)
such that
\[
h = \mathbf{1}_{I_1 \cup I_2}.
\]
For a sorted sample, the set of indices \(i\) with \(x_i \in I_1\) is either
empty or a contiguous block of indices, because an interval cannot leave and
then re-enter the ordered sample. The same is true for \(I_2\). Therefore the
set of indices with \(h(x_i)=1\) is the union of at most two contiguous blocks.
Equivalently, the binary string \(h(x_1)\dots h(x_n)\) has at most two runs of
\(1\)s.

Now suppose a binary string \(y_1\dots y_n\) has at most two runs of \(1\)s.
We construct a hypothesis in \(\classA\) realizing it.

If there are no runs of \(1\)s, then the all-zero labeling is realized by the
empty union of intervals.

If there is exactly one run of \(1\)s, say from indices \(a\) through \(b\),
then choose any interval containing \(x_a,\dots,x_b\) and excluding all other
sample points, for example
\[
I_1 =
\left[\frac{x_{a-1}+x_a}{2},\,\frac{x_b+x_{b+1}}{2}\right]
\]
with the obvious endpoint modifications when \(a=1\) or \(b=n\). Then
\(\mathbf{1}_{I_1}(x_i)=y_i\) for all \(i\), so the labeling is realized by one
interval.

If there are exactly two runs of \(1\)s, say on index blocks
\([a,b]\) and \([c,d]\) with \(b<c\), then define intervals \(I_1\) and \(I_2\)
separately around those two blocks:
\[
I_1 =
\left[\frac{x_{a-1}+x_a}{2},\,\frac{x_b+x_{b+1}}{2}\right],
\qquad
I_2 =
\left[\frac{x_{c-1}+x_c}{2},\,\frac{x_d+x_{d+1}}{2}\right],
\]
again with endpoint modifications when a block touches the sample boundary.
These intervals realize exactly those two runs and no others, so
\[
\mathbf{1}_{I_1 \cup I_2}(x_i)=y_i
\qquad \text{for all } i.
\]

Thus every labeling with at most two runs of \(1\)s is realizable by
\(\classA\), and every realizable labeling has at most two runs of \(1\)s.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
The restriction patterns of \(\classA\) are exactly the binary strings with at
most two runs of \(1\)s. This characterization is the foundation for the
growth-function count in Problem A2 and the VC-dimension computation in Problem
A3.

\subsection{Problem A2}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Using the characterization from Problem A1, compute the growth function
\(\growth_{\classA}(n)\), the number of distinct labelings of \(n\) ordered
points realizable by \(\classA\). The count must be broken into the exact cases
of no runs, one run, and two runs of \(1\)s.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is combinatorial counting. Since Problem A1 gives a complete
description of realizable labelings, the growth function is obtained by counting
binary strings with at most two runs of \(1\)s and making sure the cases do not
overlap.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Count the unique string with no \(1\)-run.
  \item Count strings with exactly one run of \(1\)s by choosing the start and
  end of the block.
  \item Count strings with exactly two runs of \(1\)s by choosing four boundary
  positions.
  \item Add the three disjoint case counts and simplify.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}

\begin{claim}
For \(n \ge 1\),
\[
\growth_{\classA}(n)
=
1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n+1}{4}.
\]
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}
By Problem A1, realizable labelings are exactly the strings with no run of
\(1\)s, exactly one run of \(1\)s, or exactly two runs of \(1\)s.

\textbf{Case 1: no runs of \(1\)s.}
There is exactly one such labeling, namely \(00\cdots0\). This contributes
\[
1.
\]

\textbf{Case 2: exactly one run of \(1\)s.}
Such a labeling is determined by choosing indices \(a \le b\) and declaring
\[
y_i =
\begin{cases}
1, & a \le i \le b,\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]
So the number of one-run strings is the number of contiguous blocks in
\(\{1,\dots,n\}\):
\[
\sum_{a=1}^n \sum_{b=a}^n 1
=
\frac{n(n+1)}{2}
=
\binom{n+1}{2}.
\]

\textbf{Case 3: exactly two runs of \(1\)s.}
Such a string has the form
\[
0\cdots 0\, 1\cdots 1\, 0\cdots 0\, 1\cdots 1\, 0\cdots 0,
\]
where the two \(1\)-runs are nonempty and separated by at least one zero. A
standard boundary encoding counts these strings by choosing four cut positions
from the \(n+1\) gaps before, between, and after the sample points. Concretely,
choose
\[
0 \le s_1 < t_1 < s_2 < t_2 \le n,
\]
and place \(1\)s on indices \(s_1+1,\dots,t_1\) and \(s_2+1,\dots,t_2\). This
is a bijection between two-run labelings and \(4\)-element subsets of the
\(n+1\) boundary positions. Therefore the number of two-run strings is
\[
\binom{n+1}{4}.
\]

Since the three cases are disjoint and exhaustive,
\[
\growth_{\classA}(n)
=
1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n+1}{4}.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
The exact growth function is
\[
\growth_{\classA}(n)
=
1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n+1}{4}.
\]
Its leading term is quartic, which already suggests the VC dimension will be
finite and fairly small.

\subsection{Problem A3}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Determine the VC dimension of \(\classA\), prove that four points can be
shattered, prove that five points cannot be shattered, and then compute the
Halving mistake bound \(\log_2 \growth_{\classA}(n)\). Finally compare that
exact Halving bound with the Sauer--Shelah upper bound.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is VC dimension. A class shatters a set when it realizes every
binary labeling on that set. The run characterization from Problem A1 makes the
shattering test especially transparent on ordered points.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Show every labeling of four ordered points has at most two runs of
  \(1\)s, so four points are shattered.
  \item Exhibit a labeling of five ordered points with three runs of \(1\)s, so
  five points are not shattered.
  \item Conclude \(\vcdim(\classA)=4\).
  \item Substitute the exact growth function from A2 into the Halving bound and
  compare it to the Sauer--Shelah bound for VC dimension \(4\).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}

\begin{claim}
\(\vcdim(\classA)=4\).
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}
Take any four ordered points \(x_1<x_2<x_3<x_4\). Every binary string of length
four has at most two runs of \(1\)s, because three runs would require at least
five positions. By Problem A1, every one of the \(2^4=16\) labelings is
realizable by \(\classA\). Hence the set \(\{x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4\}\) is shattered,
so
\[
\vcdim(\classA) \ge 4.
\]

Now consider any five ordered points \(x_1<\cdots<x_5\). The labeling
\[
(1,0,1,0,1)
\]
has three runs of \(1\)s. By Problem A1, it is not realizable by a union of at
most two intervals. Therefore no set of five ordered points is shattered, so
\[
\vcdim(\classA) \le 4.
\]
Combining the two inequalities gives
\[
\vcdim(\classA)=4.
\]
\end{proof}

For the Halving algorithm on \(n\) ordered sample points, the exact number of
candidate restrictions is \(\growth_{\classA}(n)\), so the exact Halving bound
is
\[
M_{\mathrm{Halving}}
\le
\log_2 \growth_{\classA}(n)
=
\log_2\!\left(1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n+1}{4}\right).
\]

Since \(\vcdim(\classA)=4\), the Sauer--Shelah lemma gives the general upper
bound
\[
\growth_{\classA}(n)
\le
\sum_{k=0}^4 \binom{n}{k}.
\]
Therefore a coarser Halving bound is
\[
M_{\mathrm{Halving}}
\le
\log_2\!\left(\sum_{k=0}^4 \binom{n}{k}\right).
\]

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
The VC dimension is exactly \(4\). The exact Halving mistake bound is
\[
\log_2\!\left(1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n+1}{4}\right),
\]
while Sauer--Shelah gives a more general but looser upper bound through
\(\sum_{k=0}^4 \binom{n}{k}\).

\subsubsection{6. Lean Formalization}
Lean: NO.

\subsubsection{7. Computational Check (if applicable)}
For \(n=4\), the computed growth value is
\[
\growth_{\classA}(4)=16=2^4,
\]
which agrees with the shattering claim. For \(n=5\), the computed value is
\[
\growth_{\classA}(5)=31<32=2^5,
\]
which agrees with the failure to shatter five points.

\subsection{Problem A4}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Construct a hypothesis class whose VC dimension is exactly \(d\) and whose
growth function on \(n\) points is exactly
\[
\sum_{k=0}^{d} \binom{n}{k}.
\]
Explain why this shows that the Sauer--Shelah bound can be tight.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is a tight example for Sauer--Shelah. A natural class is the
collection of subsets of the domain of size at most \(d\). On an \(n\)-point
sample, every labeling with at most \(d\) ones is realizable, and no labeling
with more than \(d\) ones is.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Define the class of all indicator functions of subsets of size at most
  \(d\).
  \item Prove that any \(d\)-point set is shattered.
  \item Prove that no \((d+1)\)-point set is shattered.
  \item Count the realizable restrictions on an \(n\)-point sample.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}

Let the domain \(X\) be any infinite set, and define
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\le d}
=
\{\mathbf{1}_S : S \subseteq X,\ |S| \le d\}.
\]

\begin{claim}
\(\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\le d})=d\).
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}
Take any \(d\) distinct points \(x_1,\dots,x_d\). For every labeling of these
points, the positively labeled subset has size at most \(d\), so it is itself a
set \(S\) with \(|S| \le d\). Then \(\mathbf{1}_S\) realizes that labeling.
Hence every \(d\)-point set is shattered, so
\[
\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\le d}) \ge d.
\]

Now take any \(d+1\) distinct points \(x_1,\dots,x_{d+1}\). The all-ones
labeling would require a set \(S\) containing all \(d+1\) sample points, but no
hypothesis in \(\mathcal{H}_{\le d}\) has support larger than \(d\). So a
\((d+1)\)-point set cannot be shattered, and
\[
\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\le d}) \le d.
\]
Therefore
\[
\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\le d})=d.
\]
\end{proof}

Now fix an \(n\)-point sample \(x_1,\dots,x_n\). A labeling is realizable by
\(\mathcal{H}_{\le d}\) if and only if it has at most \(d\) ones. For each
\(k\in\{0,\dots,d\}\), there are exactly \(\binom{n}{k}\) such labelings with
exactly \(k\) ones. Hence
\[
\growth_{\mathcal{H}_{\le d}}(n)
=
\sum_{k=0}^{d} \binom{n}{k}.
\]

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
This class has VC dimension exactly \(d\) and attains the Sauer--Shelah upper
bound exactly. Therefore the Sauer--Shelah inequality is, in general, tight.

\subsubsection{6. Lean Formalization}
Lean: NO.

\subsubsection{7. Computational Check (if applicable)}
For small \(d\) and \(n\), the expression
\(\sum_{k=0}^d \binom{n}{k}\) can be checked directly by counting the binary
strings of length \(n\) with at most \(d\) ones. No separate repository artifact
is required here because the main enumeration script already illustrates the
same type of counting argument.

\subsection{Problem A5}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Audit an AI-generated proof about the class \(\classA\). Identify the flaw in
its counting argument, explain why the proof is invalid, and replace it with a
correct statement based on the two-run characterization.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is structural counting. A bad AI proof often counts possible
``switch points'' as though label changes could occur arbitrarily, but unions of
two intervals impose strong interval constraints. The proof must respect the
geometry of the class.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item State the flaw: arbitrary switch-point counting ignores interval
  structure.
  \item Explain why this leads to overcounting.
  \item Replace the invalid claim with the correct run-based characterization.
  \item Deduce the correct growth-function statement from Problem A2.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}
Suppose the AI argument says that a union of two intervals can be counted by
choosing up to four switch points independently among the \(n-1\) gaps between
sample points, and therefore claims a count on the order of
\[
\sum_{k=0}^4 \binom{n-1}{k}.
\]
This argument is flawed for two reasons.

First, it treats label changes as arbitrary, but a union of two intervals does
not produce an arbitrary set of switch points. The positive region on a sorted
sample must be the union of at most two contiguous blocks, so the valid patterns
are exactly those with at most two runs of \(1\)s.

Second, even when a pattern has at most four changes, the locations are not
independent choices. The interval structure forces them to occur in a very
specific alternating order, and the relevant combinatorial object is not ``all
subsets of at most four switch points'' but rather ``boundary encodings of at
most two runs of \(1\)s''.

The correct statement is:
\begin{quote}
For \(n\) ordered sample points, the realizable labelings of \(\classA\) are
exactly the binary strings with at most two runs of \(1\)s. Consequently,
\[
\growth_{\classA}(n)
=
1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n+1}{4}.
\]
\end{quote}

This follows immediately from Problem A1 for the structural characterization and
from Problem A2 for the exact count.

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
The AI proof is invalid because it counts unconstrained switch patterns instead
of interval-consistent runs. The correct reasoning is run-based, not arbitrary
change-point counting.

Lean: NO.

\subsubsection{7. Computational Check (if applicable)}
The enumeration script provides a concrete reality check: for \(n \le 6\), the
brute-force counts agree with the two-run formula and do not agree with the much
larger naive switch-point count.

\section{Part B}

Throughout Part B, define the quadratic-threshold class
\[
\classB
=
\left\{
h_{a,b,c} : x \mapsto \mathbf{1}\!\left[a x^2 + b x + c \ge 0\right]
: (a,b,c)\in\R^3 \setminus \{(0,0,0)\}
\right\}.
\]
We again study restrictions to ordered samples \(x_1<\cdots<x_n\).

\subsection{Problem B1}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Prove the general upper bound: if a hypothesis class can be represented as
homogeneous linear threshold functions in \(\R^D\), then its VC dimension is at
most \(D\).

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is linear dependence. A set of \(D+1\) vectors in \(\R^D\) is
always linearly dependent. That dependence can be converted into a labeling that
no homogeneous linear threshold can realize, giving the contradiction needed for
the VC-dimension upper bound.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Assume \(D+1\) points are shattered after applying the representation
  map \(\phi\).
  \item Use linear dependence among their feature vectors.
  \item Turn that dependence relation into a forbidden labeling.
  \item Conclude that shattering \(D+1\) points is impossible.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}

\begin{theorem}
Let \(X\) be any domain and let \(\phi : X \to \R^D\). Define
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\phi}
=
\left\{
x \mapsto \mathbf{1}\!\left[\langle w,\phi(x)\rangle \ge 0\right]
: w \in \R^D
\right\}.
\]
Then
\[
\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\phi}) \le D.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume for contradiction that \(\mathcal{H}_{\phi}\) shatters
\(D+1\) points \(x_1,\dots,x_{D+1}\in X\). Write
\[
v_i = \phi(x_i)\in\R^D.
\]
Since \(D+1\) vectors in \(\R^D\) are linearly dependent, there exists a
nontrivial relation
\[
\alpha_1 v_1 + \cdots + \alpha_{D+1} v_{D+1}=0
\]
with coefficients not all zero. Remove any zero coefficients if necessary and
replace the relation by a minimal one, so every remaining coefficient is
nonzero.

Define the target labeling on the remaining indexed points by
\[
y_i =
\begin{cases}
1, & \alpha_i > 0,\\
0, & \alpha_i < 0.
\end{cases}
\]
Because the original set is shattered, this labeling must be realizable by some
weight vector \(w \in \R^D\). Since the sample is finite, we may perturb \(w\)
slightly if necessary so that the realizing inequalities are strict on the
sample:
\[
\langle w,v_i\rangle > 0 \quad \text{when } \alpha_i>0,
\qquad
\langle w,v_i\rangle < 0 \quad \text{when } \alpha_i<0.
\]
Now take the dot product of \(w\) with the dependence relation:
\[
0
=
\left\langle
w,\sum_{i=1}^{D+1}\alpha_i v_i
\right\rangle
=
\sum_{i=1}^{D+1}\alpha_i \langle w,v_i\rangle.
\]
Every term in the final sum is strictly positive, because \(\alpha_i\) and
\(\langle w,v_i\rangle\) have the same sign. Hence the sum is strictly
positive, a contradiction.

Therefore no set of \(D+1\) points can be shattered, so
\[
\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\phi}) \le D.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
Any class represented by homogeneous linear threshold functions in \(\R^D\) has
VC dimension at most \(D\). This is the general fact needed in Problem B2.

\subsubsection{6. Lean Formalization}
Lean: YES.

Target theorem to formalize:

\[
\text{If } \mathcal{H}_{\phi}
=
\left\{
x \mapsto \mathbf{1}\!\left[\langle w,\phi(x)\rangle \ge 0\right]
: w \in \R^D
\right\},
\text{ then }
\vcdim(\mathcal{H}_{\phi}) \le D.
\]
\end{notebox}

Repository artifact: \texttt{hw2/lean/vc\_dimension\_bound.lean}.

\subsubsection{7. Computational Check (if applicable)}
No numerical check is needed here because the argument is purely linear
algebraic.

\subsection{Problem B2}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Show that quadratic threshold functions on the real line admit a linear
representation in \(\R^3\) under the map
\[
\phi(x) = (x^2,x,1),
\]
and conclude from Problem B1 that their VC dimension is at most \(3\).

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is feature lifting. The quadratic polynomial
\(a x^2 + b x + c\) becomes a dot product with the lifted feature vector
\((x^2,x,1)\). Once that representation is established, Problem B1 applies
immediately.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Define the feature map \(\phi(x)=(x^2,x,1)\).
  \item Rewrite every quadratic polynomial as a dot product in \(\R^3\).
  \item Identify the quadratic-threshold class with a linear-threshold class in
  \(\R^3\).
  \item Invoke Problem B1 to obtain the VC-dimension upper bound.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}
Define
\[
\phi : \R \to \R^3,
\qquad
\phi(x) = (x^2,x,1).
\]
For any \((a,b,c)\in\R^3\), let
\[
w=(a,b,c)\in\R^3.
\]
Then
\[
\langle w,\phi(x)\rangle
=
a x^2 + b x + c.
\]
Therefore
\[
h_{a,b,c}(x)
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left[a x^2 + b x + c \ge 0\right]
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left[\langle w,\phi(x)\rangle \ge 0\right].
\]
So \(\classB\) is a linear-threshold class in \(\R^3\). By Problem B1,
\[
\vcdim(\classB) \le 3.
\]

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
Quadratic thresholds on \(\R\) lift to homogeneous linear thresholds in
\(\R^3\), and therefore
\[
\vcdim(\classB)\le 3.
\]

\subsubsection{6. Lean Formalization}
Lean: YES.

Target theorem to formalize:

\[
\forall a,b,c,x \in \R,\qquad
a x^2 + b x + c
=
\langle (a,b,c),(x^2,x,1)\rangle.
\]
Together with Problem B1, this yields \(\vcdim(\classB)\le 3\).
\end{notebox}

\subsection{Problem B3}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Describe exactly which binary labelings of an ordered sample
\(x_1<\cdots<x_n\) can arise from quadratic thresholds. The required argument
must use the fact that a quadratic has at most two real roots and must describe
all valid patterns carefully.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is root structure. A quadratic polynomial can only change sign
when crossing a real root, and it has at most two real roots. Consequently the
set on which the quadratic is nonnegative is either an interval, the complement
of an interval, all of \(\R\), or the empty set. On an ordered sample this
translates into a precise family of binary strings.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Use the root description of quadratic polynomials.
  \item Translate each geometric possibility into a binary pattern on a sorted
  sample.
  \item Show that the valid \(1\)-sets are exactly: empty, one contiguous run,
  or a prefix together with a suffix.
  \item Verify the converse by constructing an appropriate quadratic in each
  case.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}

\begin{theorem}
For an ordered sample \(x_1<\cdots<x_n\), a binary labeling
\((y_1,\dots,y_n)\in\zeroone^n\) is realizable by \(\classB\) if and only if
the set of indices with label \(1\) is one of the following:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item the empty set,
  \item a single contiguous block,
  \item the union of a prefix and a suffix separated by a nonempty middle block
  of zeros.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(q(x)=a x^2 + b x + c\).

If \(a=0\), then \(q\) is linear or constant, so the set \(\{x:q(x)\ge 0\}\) is
either empty, all of \(\R\), or a half-line. On an ordered sample this produces
either the empty labeling, the all-ones labeling, a prefix of ones, or a suffix
of ones. These are already covered by the three stated cases.

Now assume \(a\neq 0\). A quadratic has at most two real roots.

If \(q\) has no real roots, then its sign never changes, so the labeling is
either all ones or all zeros.

If \(q\) has two real roots \(r_1 \le r_2\), then:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item when \(a<0\), the set \(\{x:q(x)\ge 0\}\) is the interval
  \([r_1,r_2]\), so the \(1\)-labels form one contiguous block on the sample;
  \item when \(a>0\), the set \(\{x:q(x)\ge 0\}\) is the exterior region
  \((-\infty,r_1]\cup[r_2,\infty)\), so the \(1\)-labels form a prefix together
  with a suffix.
\end{itemize}

This proves that every realizable labeling has one of the three stated forms.

For the converse, we explicitly realize each form.

The empty labeling is realized by \(q(x)=-1\), and the all-ones labeling by
\(q(x)=1\).

If the \(1\)-labels form a single contiguous block from \(x_a\) through \(x_b\),
choose roots \(r_1< x_a\) and \(r_2> x_b\) lying between neighboring sample
points so that
\[
q(x)=-(x-r_1)(x-r_2)
\]
is nonnegative exactly on that block.

If the \(1\)-labels form a prefix together with a suffix, with a middle zero
block from \(x_a\) through \(x_b\), choose roots \(r_1< x_a\) and \(r_2> x_b\)
again between neighboring sample points, and define
\[
q(x)=(x-r_1)(x-r_2).
\]
Then \(q(x)\ge 0\) exactly outside the interval \((r_1,r_2)\), so it realizes
that prefix--suffix pattern.

Thus the listed patterns are exactly the realizable restrictions of
\(\classB\).
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
Quadratic thresholds do not produce arbitrary sign-change patterns. Their
restrictions are controlled by the root structure of quadratics: the \(1\)-set
is either empty, one block, or a prefix together with a suffix.

\subsection{Problem B4}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Use the structural characterization from Problem B3 to compute the growth
function of \(\classB\) on \(n\) ordered points, determine its VC dimension, and
compare the exact growth count with the Sauer--Shelah bound.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is again careful case counting. From B3, the valid labelings are
empty, one run, or a prefix together with a suffix. Counting those patterns
gives the exact growth function and also reveals the exact VC dimension.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Count the empty labeling.
  \item Count one-run labelings by choosing a contiguous block.
  \item Count prefix--suffix labelings by choosing the interior zero block.
  \item Sum the disjoint cases and simplify.
  \item Check shattering at \(n=3\) and failure at \(n=4\).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}
By Problem B3, the realizable labelings fall into three disjoint classes.

\textbf{Case 1: empty labeling.}
There is exactly one such labeling.

\textbf{Case 2: one contiguous run of \(1\)s.}
The number of contiguous blocks in \(n\) ordered positions is
\[
\binom{n+1}{2}.
\]
This count includes the all-ones labeling.

\textbf{Case 3: prefix together with suffix.}
Such a labeling is determined by a nonempty contiguous middle block of zeros
that is strictly interior to the sample. If that zero block runs from index
\(a\) to index \(b\), then
\[
2 \le a \le b \le n-1.
\]
The number of such interior zero blocks is the number of contiguous blocks in
\(\{2,\dots,n-1\}\), namely
\[
\binom{n-1}{2}.
\]

Adding the cases gives
\[
\growth_{\classB}(n)
=
1 + \binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n-1}{2}.
\]
Since
\[
\binom{n+1}{2} + \binom{n-1}{2}
=
\frac{n(n+1)}{2} + \frac{(n-1)(n-2)}{2}
=
n^2-n+1,
\]
we obtain the closed form
\[
\growth_{\classB}(n)=n^2-n+2.
\]

To compute the VC dimension, observe first that
\[
\growth_{\classB}(3)=3^2-3+2=8=2^3.
\]
So three ordered points are shattered. On the other hand,
\[
\growth_{\classB}(4)=4^2-4+2=14<16=2^4,
\]
so four points are not shattered. Hence
\[
\vcdim(\classB)=3.
\]

This matches the upper bound from Problem B2. Since \(\vcdim(\classB)=3\),
Sauer--Shelah gives
\[
\growth_{\classB}(n)
\le
\sum_{k=0}^{3}\binom{n}{k}.
\]
The exact count \(n^2-n+2\) is sharper for this specific class.

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
The exact growth function is
\[
\growth_{\classB}(n)=n^2-n+2,
\]
and the VC dimension is exactly \(3\). The exact count is smaller than the
general Sauer--Shelah upper bound, as expected.

\subsection{Problem B5}

\subsubsection{1. Restatement}
Audit an AI proof about quadratic thresholds. Identify the flaw in an argument
that counts arbitrary change points, explain why that argument is invalid, and
replace it with correct reasoning based on the root structure of quadratics.

\subsubsection{2. Explanation}
The key concept is that not every pattern with a small number of sign changes is
produced by a quadratic. The shape of a quadratic forces the positive region to
be either an interval or the complement of an interval. Any proof that ignores
this curvature constraint overcounts the hypothesis class.

\subsubsection{3. Solution Plan}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item Identify the flaw: arbitrary change-point counting ignores the shape of
  quadratic level sets.
  \item Explain the missing constraint: a quadratic has at most two roots and a
  fixed opening direction.
  \item Replace the argument with the interval / complement-of-interval
  classification from Problem B3.
  \item Deduce the correct growth function from Problem B4.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4. Formal Solution / Proof}
Suppose an AI argument claims that because a quadratic has at most two roots, we
can choose up to two sign-change locations arbitrarily among the \(n-1\) sample
gaps, and therefore count all strings with at most two changes. This is
incorrect.

The flaw is that ``at most two changes'' is necessary but not sufficient for a
quadratic threshold. The polynomial shape matters. For example, a pattern like
\[
0,1,1,0
\]
has two changes and is realizable, but a pattern like
\[
1,0,0,1
\]
is also realizable for a completely different geometric reason: it comes from a
quadratic that opens upward and is nonnegative outside an interval. Counting
``change points'' alone misses this distinction and does not encode whether the
quadratic is selecting an interval or its complement.

The correct statement is the one proved in Problem B3: on an ordered sample, the
realisable \(1\)-sets are exactly the empty set, one contiguous block, or a
prefix together with a suffix. Consequently, by Problem B4,
\[
\growth_{\classB}(n)=n^2-n+2.
\]

\subsubsection{5. Conclusion}
The invalid AI proof overcounts by ignoring the interval structure imposed by
quadratic roots. The right argument is root-based and shape-based, not arbitrary
change-point counting.
